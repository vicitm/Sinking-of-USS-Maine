Background

On February 15, 1898, the USS Maine, an American battleship, exploded and sank in Havana Harbor, Cuba, killing 266 sailors. The incident sparked outrage in the United States and led to the outbreak of the Spanish-American War. The exact cause of the explosion remains a mystery, but it is believed to have been either an external mine or an internal fire. Regardless of the cause, the sinking of the Maine played a significant role in shaping American foreign policy and marked a turning point in the nation's history.

At the time of the sinking of the Maine, Cuba was in the midst of a prolonged and bloody rebellion against Spanish colonial rule. The United States had a long history of involvement in Cuban affairs, and many Americans sympathized with the Cuban rebels. Timeline

1. January 24, 1898

President McKinley Orders USS Maine to Havana Harbor

President William McKinley orders the USS Maine, a second-class battleship, to Havana Harbor to protect American citizens and interests amidst the escalating tensions in Cuba.

2. February 15, 1898

Explosion Sinks USS Maine

At 9:40 PM, a massive explosion rocks the USS Maine in Havana Harbor, tearing apart the ship and sending it to the bottom of the harbor. The explosion kills 268 sailors and injures several others.

3. February 16, 1898

Naval Court of Inquiry Appointed

President McKinley appoints a naval court of inquiry to investigate the cause of the USS Maine explosion.

4. March 21, 1898

Cause of Explosion Concluded

The U.S. naval court of inquiry concludes that the Maine was destroyed by an external explosion, likely triggered by a mine.

5. April 20, 1898

Ultimatum to Spain

President McKinley sends an ultimatum to Spain, demanding that it withdraw its forces from Cuba and end its rule over the island.

6. April 22, 1898

Spain Rejects Ultimatum

Spain rejects the American ultimatum.

7. April 25, 1898

United States Declares War

The United States declares war on Spain.

8. May 1, 1898

Battle of Manila Bay

Commodore George Dewey leads the American fleet to a decisive victory over the Spanish fleet in Manila Bay, Philippines.

9. July 17, 1898

American Forces Land in Cuba

American forces land in Cuba and begin a campaign to capture the island.

10. August 12, 1898

Armistice Signed

An armistice is signed between the United States and Spain, ending the Spanish-American War.

Impact of the Sinking of USS Maine

The sinking of the USS Maine in 1898 had profound consequences, both in terms of its immediate aftermath and the broader historical context.

The event heightened tensions between the United States and Spain, ultimately leading to the outbreak of the Spanish-American War. The war, fueled by public anger and the rallying cry "Remember the Maine," resulted in a decisive American victory and marked the end of Spain's colonial empire.

Yellow journalism played a significant role in shaping public opinion and pushing the nation towards war. The sensationalized reporting by newspapers, particularly those owned by William Randolph Hearst and Joseph Pulitzer, contributed to the fervor and desire for retaliation.

The sinking of the Maine, whether caused by an internal explosion or an external mine, became a symbol of American resolve and a catalyst for military action. The war that ensued had far-reaching geopolitical implications, solidifying the United States as a major player on the world stage.
